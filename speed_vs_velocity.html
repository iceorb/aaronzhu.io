Speed vs. Velocity

Feb 17, 2026 · 5 min read

I judged a hackathon last month. Forty teams. Every single one had a working demo—polished UIs, functional backends, the whole thing. Two years ago, maybe five teams would have gotten that far in 48 hours. Now, with Cursor, Codex, and Claude Code, every team ships.

Almost none of them had a good idea.

I keep coming back to this. AI has made it trivially easy to build things. But it hasn't made it any easier to know what's worth building. If anything, it's made it harder, because the cost of building the wrong thing used to be high enough that it forced you to think first. Now you can build the wrong thing in a weekend and not realize it for months.

There's a distinction in physics that I think about a lot. Speed is a scalar—just how fast you're going. Velocity is a vector—how fast you're going and in what direction. A car doing 120mph on the wrong highway isn't making progress. It's just getting lost faster.

I scroll through Y Combinator launches and half of them feel like the same app with a different color scheme. "AI-powered X for Y." The execution is flawless. I can't tell you what problem any of them solve.

This has happened before. Blogs and social media blew open who could publish. That was real and important—a kid in Lagos could reach the same audience as the New York Times, the Arab Spring was coordinated on Facebook, communities that legacy media ignored finally had a platform. But the noise floor rose with the ceiling. Clickbait, misinformation, rage-optimized algorithms. We traded editorial friction for algorithmic reach, and the average quality of what people consumed cratered. I think we're about to do the same thing to software.

The people who ended up mattering in media weren't the ones who could publish the most—they were the ones who could tell you what was worth reading. Editors, curators, people with taste. The same resorting is about to happen in software.

There are real wins, don't get me wrong. A nurse with a workflow problem can prototype her own tool now. A student in Nairobi doesn't need a CS degree to ship. I don't want to go back to a world where you need a $150k salary to make software. But the downside is the same—a flood of stuff that works perfectly and solves nothing. Anthropic calls it the "fast fashion of software"—code that's cheap to produce and not built to endure.

At DoorDash, I watched this play out before AI even accelerated it. We could ship model iterations fast—new ranking algorithms, new features, new experiments. The iterations that actually moved the needle were never the fast ones. They were the ones where someone on the team understood something deeply specific about why a person orders food at 11pm on a Tuesday. That kind of insight doesn't come from a prompt. It comes from sitting with a problem long enough that it starts to make sense.

I think that's the thing that's actually scarce now. Not building ability—everyone has that. Knowing what to build.

Here's maybe a nerdy way to put it. Say you have 100 engineers who are cracked at execution but don't know what to build. They brute force it—idea after idea, pivot after pivot. That's O(n), linear search through the problem space. Now imagine ten engineers and one person who deeply understands the problem, who's talked to users, who's lived in the domain long enough to have taste. That person doesn't search linearly—they prune. They eliminate entire branches before a line of code is written. That's O(log n). AI is giving everyone more compute. It's not giving anyone a better algorithm.
